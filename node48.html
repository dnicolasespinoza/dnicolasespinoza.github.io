Thermo-elasticity

Changes of temperature change the equilibrium distance between molecules in solids, and therefore induce strains. Imagine a solids heated up, but not allowed to dilate in vertical direction 3.23. The result is an increase of stress in vertical direction rather than a deformation in vertical direction.

The coefficient of thermal dilation quantifies strains as a function of changes of temperature at constant pressure .

(3.36)

Under unconstrained conditions, a negative change in temperature causes shrinkage and a positive change in temperature causes dilation. The updated elastic equations make explicit that stresses can be changed as a result of a change in temperature and/or as a result of a change of volumetric strains.

(3.37)

Equation 3.37 does not include the effects of pore pressure. The coupled thermo-poro-elastic equations are covered in the Advanced Geomechanics course.

Thermal(-induced) stresses can cause reductions in fracture gradient when drilling with relatively cold drilling mud and enhanced fractured reactivation when injecting cold fluids in a hot reservoir.
